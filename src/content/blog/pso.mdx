Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) is a computational method that optimizes a problem by iteratively trying to improve candidate solutions. It's inspired by the social behavior of bird flocking or fish schooling.

The visualization above shows a swarm of particles (cyan dots) searching for the global minimum (red dashed circle) on a rugged landscape with multiple local minima (purple circles). Each particle:

1. **Remembers** its personal best position
2. **Communicates** with the swarm about the global best position
3. **Balances** exploration (random movement) and exploitation (moving toward known good positions)

Click anywhere to move the target and watch the swarm adapt. The particles will initially scatter, then gradually converge as they share information about promising regions.

---

## Why Use PSO?

PSO is particularly useful when:

- **No gradient information** is available (black-box optimization)
- The search space is **high-dimensional** and complex
- You need a **good enough** solution quickly, rather than a proven optimum
- The objective function has **multiple local minima**

It's not the best choice when you have a smooth, convex objective with known gradients — in that case, classical optimization methods will be faster and more reliable.

## The Algorithm

Each particle $i$ has a position $x_i$ and velocity $v_i$. At each iteration:

$$
v_i \leftarrow w \cdot v_i + c_1 r_1 (p_i - x_i) + c_2 r_2 (g - x_i)
$$

$$
x_i \leftarrow x_i + v_i
$$

Where:
- $w$ is the **inertia weight** (how much the particle trusts its current direction)
- $c_1, c_2$ are the **cognitive and social coefficients**
- $p_i$ is the particle's **personal best** position
- $g$ is the **global best** position found by any particle
- $r_1, r_2$ are random values in $[0, 1]$

The balance between these terms determines whether the swarm explores broadly or exploits known good regions.
